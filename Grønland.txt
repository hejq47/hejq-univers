	 altsaa som ei videre var ved dette Skifte at observere, saa sluttes det..."

	og Adgangsrettigheden blev derfor ved 8 Mands Dom af 15.10. 1731 tilkendt Jep
	 Hansens søn af andet ægteskab, den da kun 2½ år gamle Esber Ipsen. Gården
	 vurderedes til 292 Dlr.

	 For almost 40 years Jep Hansen lived at Elleskovgård in 43' Slg.
	 Østerlars parish. On December 18, 1693 the honourary county-official
	 Hans Hendrich Shaar "put Elleskovgård on the block in East District" and
	 on March 10, 1694 the above official gave a receipt for the amount owing
	 to Jens Mogensen of Østerlars, who very likely was a relative of Peder
	 Mogensen, who at the time was farming Elleskovgård. Only a few months
	 later Jens Mogensen was paid 195 Silverdaler by Jep Hansen, who proceeded
	 to take ownership of Elleskovgård on June 4, 1694.
	
	 However, in the following years quarrels erupted as to the legality of
	 the transfer of ownership: Peder Mogensen's step-daughter Lene Larsdatter
	 (later married to Peder Haumand) claimed the right of ownership to
	 Elleskovgård. The case was heard in the lower courts three times before
	 it finally was decided upon in the highest court of the land. (See below
	 for an excerpt from the case-history.)
	
	 In the Inheritance-Protocol Jep Hansen's brother, Esber Hansen, is noted
	 as living in Østerlars parish. According to records from 1689 and 1719 a
	 man bearing this name was living at Kjelseby-gårde. He died on September
	 18, 1729 at 73 years of age. Furthermore, according to the
	 Inheritance-Protocols a nephew of Jep Hansen's is mentioned to be living
	 at Kjelseby, Østerlars parish from 1729-33; he died in 1770, 82 years
	 old, but I have not been able to prove that they were related.
	
	 Jep Hansen, who was buried April 18, 1731 at about 80 years old, was
	 twice married: first to Ellen Pedersdatter and a second time, when he
	 was about 62, to Kirsten Berildsdatter, who was 30 years old. He had 12
	 children from these two marriages.

	 At the inheritance proceedings upon the death of Jep Hansen, there was a
	 great deal of disagreement between the children as to the just division
	 of the property. Ironically, since there was nothing left to be divided
	 after the debts were paid, that was the end of the wranglings! However,
	 the rights to Elleskovgård were yet to be settled, so a jury of eight men
	 on October 15, 1731 gave the right to Jep Hansen's son from his second
	 marriage -- the two year old Esper Ibsen. (As he was the youngest son,
	 this is in line with Bornholm's inheritance laws.) The farm was valued
	 at 292 Dlr. Kirsten Berildsdatter got re-married, to Anders Svendsen,
	 who took over the operation of the farm.
	
	 In the Land-Registries for 1691, 1696, 1719 and 1773 Elleskovgård was
	 valued at 12 Td. 1 Skp. 2 Fdk. 0 Alb. and 69/130 Pt. Hartkorn (wheat),
	 whereupon a tax of 8 Rdl. 2 Mk. 13 Sk. and 1-1/2 Alb. was lain.
	
	 An excerpt of the case on the disputed rights to Elleskovgård:
	 Justice Morten Jensen refers in his judgement on June 10, 1716 to the
	 decisions of the two previous Lower-Court judgements (as decided by
	 Ancher Müller on November 7, 1694 and by Mathis Rask on February 13,
	 1695) and concludes:
	
	 "The ruling by the first judge, Ancher Müller, recognized Jep Hansen's
	 purchase of the farm from Peder Mogensen and his wife. Although if their
	 step-daughter Lene Larsdatter and her husband were able to come up with
	 the purchase price, Jep Hansen would have to hand over the farm.
	 However, the above clause was over-ruled by the next judge, Mathis Rask.
	 It is my ruling that the original decision stand its ground."
	
	 However, the quarrel continued, and the main bone of contention pertained
	 to a receipt signed by Peder Haumand on behalf of his wife - for her
	 inheritance money. He claimed that he did not sign away his wife's right
	 to inherit Elleskovgård. Regarding this claim Justice Morten Jensen
	 states the following:
	
	 "From the receipt of March 1, 1708 you see that Peder Haumand has not
	 signed for more than his wife's inheritance money, not releasing the
	 right to purchase Elleskovgård from Jep Hansen or anybody else."
	 Furthermore he adds: "since above mentioned Jep Hansen can not show the
	 appropriate documents proving that Peder Haumand gave him the right to
	 purchase or actually sold him Elleskovgård on behalf of his wife, then
	 according to the ruling of judge Ancher Müller on November 7, 1694 the
	 right to ownership of above farm is to be awarded to Peder Haumand, as
	 long as he is able to come up with the full purchase price within six
	 weeks from the date of this ruling." Jep Hansen had to pay 6 Rdlr. in
	 expenses for the court proceedings.
	
	 A year later the case was re-opened in the Highest-Court, the following
	 summary was entered in the Court-Protocol:
	
	 "Jep Hansen, residing at the 43rd farm in Østerlarsker (Elleskovgård)
	 versus Peder Haumand, residing in Gudhjem: This court is herewith
	 challenging the decision made by judge Morten Jensen in the Aakirkeby
	 court proceeding of June 10, 1716, whereby Jep Hansen was ordered to
	 transfer the right of ownership of Elleskovgård to Peder Haumand; and
	 further this High-Court has found proof that Jep Hansen, in full
	 agreement with the law, has bought and paid in total for above farm, and
	 furthermore, has restored Elleskovgård from its run-down state during his
	 22 years of occupancy. Thus it is considered just that Peder Haumand pay
	 for the expenses of above court proceedings."
	
	 This last was clause was contested by Peder Haumand. He wanted the court
	 to cancel above payment, considering the many advantages bestowed on Jep
	 Hansen during his 22 years residence on the farm!
	
	 The next entry in the Court-Protocol is the verdict of the nine
	 High-Court judges, starting with an entry from the Mayor of København,
	 Jacob Sidenborg, who says:
	
	 "On June 4, 1694 Peder Mogensen transferred the right to purchase
	 Elleskovgård to Jep Hansen, who however, stays this right to Lene
	 Larsdatter until July 3, 1694. The following year the remaining heirs
	 forego their rights to the farm - except Lene Larsdatter. In 1707 she
	 marries Peder Haumand and thus he attains her right of access to above
	 farm, but not for long, on March 1, 1708 he relinquishes all rights
	 without reservations. Now, however, he claims that he signed the release
	 in a drunken stupor; but since he waited eight long years to mention this
	 predicament instead of rectifying the matter the next day - no
	 consideration can be taken to his claim. The farm has now for 20 years
	 legally belonged to Jep Hansen, he is free of accusation. Peder Haumand
	 has to pay him 50 Silverdaler for expenses."
	
	 However, the next three judges rule against Jep Hansen: the King's
	 Council Loren Kreyer does not accept that Peder Haumand relinquished his
	 right to the farm on the signed receipt, and thus considers Lene
	 Larsdatter and Peder Haumand the rightful heirs to Elleskovgård; also Jep
	 Hansen has to pay the 30 to 40 Silverdaler expenses. Another King's
	 Council, Hans Vilhelm Kaalund and the Judge for the Admiralty-Court Niels
	 Foss are of the same opinion as Kreyer.
	
	 The remaining five judges vote in favour of Jep Hansen. University of
	 København professor Christian Reitzer says: "Jep Hansen has proceeded
	 according to the law. He has been the legal owner of the farm for 21
	 years. Peder Haumand relinquished all rights when signing the receipt.
	 He has to pay 80 Silverdaler in expenses."
	
	 King's Council Christian Scavenius finds that Lene Larsdatter, or her
	 mother - who was still alive at the signing of the receipt, had no
	 further right to the farm, since there was no mention of her claim in the
	 document. Peder Haumand could swear on the Bible that he was drunk when
	 he signed, but it is against the law to win a court case in this manner.
	 Jep Hansen is found not guilty and is to receive 60 to 80 Silverdaler.
	
	 The next two judges, Præstegaard and Bartholin, agreed completely, and
	 finally the highest judge, Casper Schøller: "Votes the same way, but
	 cancels the expenses to be paid, because he did not find Jep Hansen
	 absolutely fair in his dealings regarding the release of the rights to
	 the farm."
	
	 Of the nine High-Court judges six voted in favour of Jep Hansen, and thus
	 he won his case, and many years of quarrelling over the rights to
	 Elleskovgård finally came to an end.

9344.	Michel Sort #391, * inden 1500, † 1560 i Sortegård, Åker, Sønder Herred, Bornholms Amt.  ?Michel Sort was parish clergyman in Åker from 1520 to 1560. He ownd Sortegård, 3' Slg. Åker parish. On Bornholm the farms (in Danish 'gård', or old style 'gaard') had long standing official names and numbers, and they were divided into three classifications, in descending order: Proprietairgård (Propr.), earlier called Friegård; Selvejergård (Slg.); and Vaarnedergård, also spelled Vornedegård (Vdg.). A map drawn in 1851 shows 17 estate-farms classified as Proprietair: 3 in each of Åker, Ibsker and Nyker parishes; 2 each in Østermarie and Klemensker; and 1 each in Pedersker, Bodilsker, Østerlars, and Olsker. There were hundreds of farms comprising the other two classifications. The typical farm is arranged in a joined U shape, with the farmhouse, barn, pig-stall, and utility-shed all built around a cobble-stone courtyard. The church for Åkirkeby township and Åker parish is located just outside of the township of Åkirkeby proper, so paradoxically enough the town that draws its name from the 'River Church' (Aae Kirke) does not actually have a church within its municipal boundries at all. Åkirkeby was the traditional seat of Bornholm's judiciary authority, its 'Landsting' (Parliament), and the Aae Kirke was used as their meeting place. The following is a look at events taking place between Danmark and Lübeck during the lifetime of Michel Sort: In 1509 Danmark and Lübeck were engaged in one of their numerous skirmishes. The Danes had a famous battleship called the 'Svane' (english: Swan) - which was reputed to be the largest and most powerful in the world; but the Lübeck'ers in several small vessels surprised, attacked and destroyed it. After this victory a fleet of 14 Lübeck ships ravaged the Danish islands and did much damage to Danish shipping. Nine Swedish ships joined them, and the combined fleet almost ruined Lolland and Bornholm. Later, in the summer of 1509, a great naval battle took place between 16 Lübeck men-of-war and 17 Danish ships of about equal size and strength. One of the latter was a new vessel, the 'Engel' (english: Angel), larger than the unlucky Svane had been. The Lübeck'ers had landed some of their guns and men to attack the fortress of Hammershus, on the northern tip of Bornholm, when the Danish fleet appeared, quite unexpectedly, and attacked at once. The battle lasted all day, and at night both sides claimed the victory. Some days later the fight was resumed, when, after several hours of fierce contest, the Engel had her rudder shot away and was taken in tow by her consorts, and the whole Danish fleet fled.
